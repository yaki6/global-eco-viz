# Journalist Review: Storytelling Quality Assessment
**Reviewer:** Data Journalist (New York Times / Financial Times / The Economist background)
**Date:** 2026-03-04
**Subject:** Global Economic Visualization — Narrative & Storytelling Review

---

## Executive Summary

The visualization has strong bones: a compelling dataset, a sound four-chapter structure, and the right instinct to use scrollytelling. But the current narrative text reads like a textbook introduction, not journalism. It tells readers *what* they are looking at rather than making them *feel* the stakes. The hero section is forgettable. The chapters lack emotional arcs. There are no moments of surprise or wonder. With targeted rewrites, this could be genuinely gripping.

**Overall storytelling grade: C+ (solid foundation, weak execution)**

---

## 1. Hero Section

### Current Text
> "Explore 150 years of macroeconomic history across 18 countries. Scroll through interactive storylines to discover the patterns that shape our economies."

### Assessment: WEAK — Does Not Hook the Reader

This is the most important sentence on the page and it reads like a museum placard. It describes the tool, not the story. It gives no reason to care. A visitor arriving from a link will read this, shrug, and close the tab.

**Problems:**
- "Explore" and "discover" are empty verbs — they ask the reader to do the work
- "Patterns that shape our economies" is vague abstraction
- No stakes. No tension. No human drama.
- The data attribution is in the wrong place — it signals "academic project" not "compelling story" right from the start

**Suggested rewrite:**
> **Why do economies collapse?**
>
> Every financial crisis in modern history was preceded by the same warning signs — a surge in lending, rising asset prices, and widespread confidence that this time is different. It never is.
>
> This is the story of boom and bust, wealth and loss, across 18 countries and 150 years of data. Scroll to begin.

**Alternative (more evocative):**
> In 1929, American banks held more debt relative to the economy than at any point in history. Then everything fell apart. The same pattern appeared in Japan in 1990, in Iceland in 2008, in Spain in 2010.
>
> These were not accidents. Explore 150 years of data to see why.

**Why this works:** Opens with a concrete historical moment, establishes a pattern, implies inevitability and therefore *stakes*, and invites the reader into a mystery.

## 2. Chapter Story Arc Quality

### Chapter 1: Credit Cycles & Financial Crises

**Structure assessment:** Setup (credit boom) → [Weak tension] → Insight (crisis follows)

**What's missing:** The *human cost* of crisis. The current text is entirely macro-systemic. Readers will not feel why a rising credit-to-GDP ratio matters. The tension should come from knowing that behind every red band is unemployment, foreclosure, and political upheaval.

**Current narrative sections:**

| Section | Arc Element | Grade | Issue |
|---------|------------|-------|-------|
| "The Credit Boom Pattern" | Setup | B | Decent setup but generic |
| "When Credit Outpaces GDP" | Setup (repeated) | C | Redundant — two setup beats, no tension |
| "The Crisis Aftermath" | Insight | C+ | Mentions devastation but abstractly |
| "Cross-Country Patterns" | Insight (repeated) | C | Should be the payoff, feels like a footnote |

**Key problem:** There are TWO setup sections and TWO insight sections. There is no tension beat — no moment where the reader thinks "wait, could this happen again?"

**Suggested narrative arc restructure:**

1. **Setup** — "The Warning Sign" — Describe the telltale pattern: credit growing faster than the economy. Make it feel ominous.
2. **Tension** — "The Point of No Return" — Show a specific historical example in sharp detail (e.g., USA 2004-2007: the chart rises, and rises, and rises...). The reader should feel dread.
3. **Crisis** — "Then Everything Stopped" — The red bands land. Losses, unemployment, political crisis. Not abstract.
4. **Pattern recognition** — "It Has Happened Before. It Will Happen Again." — Universal pattern across countries. Call to vigilance.

**Specific wording improvements:**

- Change: *"The ratio of private credit to GDP is one of the most reliable early warning signals for financial crises."*
- To: *"Economists have spent decades trying to predict financial crashes. The single most reliable warning sign? Banks lending money faster than the economy is growing."*

- Change: *"Banking crises are devastating."*
- To: *"When credit booms collapse, the wreckage is enormous. The 2008 US crisis wiped out $11 trillion in household wealth. It took a decade to recover."*

### Chapter 2: The Rate of Return on Everything

**Structure assessment:** [Essentially absent in the implementation plan]

The implementation plan for Chapter 2 is just three lines: "Multi-line chart comparing asset returns... Bar chart... Narrative about risk premium." There is no narrative arc at all. This is the chapter with the most emotionally resonant finding in the entire dataset — that housing has outperformed equities over 150 years — and it is treated as a technical description.

**Missing story arc:**

The central tension here is the *surprise finding*: conventional wisdom says stocks are the best long-term investment, but the data says houses have matched or beaten equities in most countries. This is a genuine "wait, what?" moment for non-expert readers. It should be the centerpiece.

**Suggested arc:**

1. **Setup / False premise** — "Everyone knows stocks beat everything in the long run." Introduce the $1-invested-in-1870 chart showing equity growth. Impressive.
2. **Tension / Reveal** — "Except that's not the whole story." Add housing to the chart. It nearly matches equities in most countries — with *lower volatility*.
3. **Deeper insight** — "What does this tell us about how wealth accumulates?" Housing is not just shelter; it has been the primary wealth-building vehicle for most households, including those who never owned a share of stock.
4. **Complication** — "But not everywhere, and not always." Wartime destruction, hyperinflation, nationalization — times when historical patterns broke.

**Opening line suggestion:**
> *"Ask a financial advisor where to put your life savings and they will almost certainly say: stocks. They have 150 years of data, they'll tell you. So do we."*

### Chapter 3: Fiscal Policy & Debt Sustainability

**Structure assessment:** Technical description, no arc

**Current stated focus:** "Wartime debt accumulation, peacetime consolidation, multi-country comparison"

This is actually the richest storytelling chapter if framed correctly. The visual of debt-to-GDP spiking during WWI and WWII — then governments actually paying it down — is viscerally dramatic. The story of how countries handled (or failed to handle) debt is living history.

**Missing tension:** The reader should feel the weight of these debt decisions. What were governments choosing between? Austerity vs. spending. Default vs. inflation. These were real political choices with human consequences.

**Suggested arc:**

1. **Setup** — "Governments carry debt. Every government. Always." Normalize the concept.
2. **Tension** — "Two world wars nearly broke the system." Show the spike — UK debt hit 250% of GDP after WWII. Germany's debt was effectively erased by hyperinflation. Dramatic visual.
3. **Insight** — "The remarkable postwar consensus: grow your way out." Show the steady decline from the 1950s to 1980s. This was not inevitable — it required political decisions.
4. **Modern complication** — "Since 2008, debt has climbed again. This time, interest rates are higher." Leave the reader with a question, not an answer.

**Specific language improvement:**

- Change (generic): *"Narrative about wartime finance"*
- To (specific): *"In 1945, the United Kingdom owed creditors 250% of its entire annual economic output. What happened next surprised even economists."*

### Chapter 4: Inflation & Monetary Policy

**Structure assessment:** Abstract economic concepts, no human connection

**The Phillips curve** is one of the most important and contested ideas in economics. Its story — the idea that you can trade a little inflation for less unemployment, the stagflation of the 1970s that seemed to kill the theory, the debates that followed — is genuinely fascinating and directly affects every person's life.

**Missing:** The narrative plan says "Phillips curve, monetary neutrality, interest rate policy." This is a course syllabus, not a story.

**Suggested arc:**

1. **Setup / The Promise** — "For decades, central bankers believed they had a dial: turn up inflation slightly, unemployment falls." Show the curve working beautifully in the 1950s-60s data.
2. **Tension / The Shock** — "Then came the 1970s." Oil crisis, stagflation — inflation AND unemployment rising together. The scatter plot shows the curve breaking down, points flying everywhere. Disorienting.
3. **The Response** — "Paul Volcker's brutal cure." Show interest rates spiking to 20% in 1981. Show inflation coming down — but also the recession that followed.
4. **Resolution / Unresolved question** — "The curve has shifted. But we don't fully know why." Low inflation + low unemployment in the 2010s vs. the post-2022 surge. The data raises questions it cannot fully answer.

## 3. Scroll Pacing

**Assessment:** Likely to feel rushed or uneven

Each chapter has 4 narrative sections that each occupy one full viewport height (per the `min-h-screen` layout). At typical reading speed, a paragraph takes 15-30 seconds. Four sections = roughly 2 minutes per chapter.

**Issues:**

1. **Section 2 "When Credit Outpaces GDP"** is only slightly different from Section 1. The reader will scroll, read something almost identical to what they just read, and feel that nothing changed. This is a *pacing fail*.

2. **No "breathing room" sections.** Sometimes a section should just be one powerful sentence. Example:
   > *"Between 1929 and 1933, US banks lost 40% of their deposits."*
   Full stop. Let that land. Then scroll.

3. **Chart updates are too infrequent.** Going from `credit_line` to `credit_with_crisis` is the only state change across all 4 sections. The reader scrolls through 50% of a chapter before anything changes visually. Consider having each of the 4 sections trigger a meaningful chart update.

**Recommended pacing structure per chapter:**
- Section 1: Setup — chart shows baseline (no crisis markers, one asset, simple view)
- Section 2: Complication — chart adds data (second country, crisis markers revealed, timeline extended)
- Section 3: Payoff — chart shows the dramatic moment (zoom to crisis year, annotated peak)
- Section 4: Reflection — chart zooms out to full history, pattern visible

## 4. Language Accessibility

**Assessment:** Adequate for economics students, alienating for general public

The target audience is stated as "economics students and the general public." The narrative text is appropriate for the former but will lose the latter.

**Terms that need translation:**
- "credit-to-GDP ratio" — say "how much banks are lending compared to the size of the whole economy"
- "systemic banking crisis" — say "a crisis that spreads through the whole financial system, not just one bank"
- "risk premium" — say "the extra reward investors demand for taking on risk"
- "Phillips curve" — fine to use, but immediately explain it in plain language
- "monetary neutrality" — never use this term without a plain-language parallel
- "fiscal multipliers" — explain with a concrete example before introducing the term

**Tone:** The current text is written in passive constructions and distant third person. Compare:

- Current: *"The ratio of private credit to GDP is one of the most reliable early warning signals."*
- More engaging: *"When this ratio climbs, pay attention. Something bad is usually coming."*

**Rule of thumb:** Could a curious 22-year-old with no economics background follow this story? If they'd need to google more than one term per paragraph, rewrite.

## 5. Chapter Transitions

**Assessment:** Non-existent — chapters end abruptly

There are no transition moments in the plan. Chapter 1 ends. Chapter 2 begins. The reader has no sense of how these four stories connect.

**Why this matters:** The four chapters are not independent topics. They are *interconnected systems*:
- Credit booms (Ch.1) inflate asset prices including equities and housing (Ch.2)
- Crises trigger government intervention and debt (Ch.3)
- Governments manage crises through monetary policy and inflation (Ch.4)

This is a circle, not a sequence. The transitions should make this explicit.

**Suggested transition text between chapters:**

*After Ch.1 → before Ch.2:*
> "Crises don't just damage banks. They reshape how wealth behaves — which assets survive, which collapse. In the next chapter, we look at where money has actually been safe over 150 years of boom and bust."

*After Ch.2 → before Ch.3:*
> "When private wealth collapses, governments step in. But that intervention comes at a cost — debt that can take generations to repay."

*After Ch.3 → before Ch.4:*
> "There is one tool that governments have used, consistently and controversially, to escape from debt: inflation. The final chapter examines the relationship between prices, employment, and the central bank's impossible choices."

## 6. Emotional Engagement: Moments of Surprise / Wonder

**Assessment:** Currently zero — this must be fixed

The best data journalism creates moments that stop readers cold — an unexpected number, a counterintuitive pattern, a statistic that reframes everything they thought they knew.

**Potential "wow" moments hiding in this dataset that the narrative plan ignores:**

1. **The house price revelation (Ch.2):** Housing has matched equities over 150 years. Annotate the chart with this finding. Make readers do a double-take.

2. **The war debt spike (Ch.3):** UK debt at 250% of GDP in 1945. Visualize this peak with a label and the words: *"One year of borrowing for every two years of output."* Visceral.

3. **The 1970s Phillips curve collapse (Ch.4):** Show the tidy 1960s curve, then the chaotic 1970s scatter. The *visual disintegration* of the pattern is genuinely shocking if set up well.

4. **The universal pre-crisis pattern (Ch.1):** If you can show 5 different countries' credit-to-GDP ratios *all* spiking before their respective crises, side by side, the universality is haunting.

5. **The Great Moderation (implied in Ch.4):** From 1985-2007, most developed economies experienced stable growth, low inflation, and low unemployment simultaneously — something historically unprecedented. Then 2008 happened.

**Technique for creating wonder:** Use annotation on charts. When a line reaches its historical peak, a label should appear: *"USA, 2007: Credit at its highest point since the Great Depression."* The number makes it real.

## 7. Call-to-Action: Does the Site Inspire Further Exploration?

**Assessment:** No CTA exists — significant missed opportunity

The current plan has a footer with data attribution, but no invitation to go deeper. After spending 20 minutes with this data, a reader should feel:
- Curious to investigate their own country
- Equipped to spot warning signs in current economic news
- Inspired to read more

**Suggested additions:**

1. **Interactive cross-chapter explorer** — "After reading, you can explore any country's full 150-year history across all four dimensions." Link to an open-ended explorer mode.

2. **"What does this mean today?" callout at the end of each chapter** — A brief paragraph connecting the historical patterns to a current event. Example for Ch.1:
   > *"As of 2024, household debt in South Korea has reached 104% of GDP — among the highest in the dataset. Watch this space."*

3. **Data download link** — Serious readers will want to explore the underlying data. Link to the JST database. This builds trust and signals intellectual honesty.

4. **Further reading** — Three to five books or papers for readers who want to go deeper. The Economist's *"When did economics go wrong?"* style list.

5. **Closing statement** that gives readers something to carry with them:
   > *"One hundred and fifty years of data tell us one thing clearly: economies cycle between optimism and crisis, between boom and collapse. Understanding the pattern does not guarantee we can stop it. But it makes us better equipped to see it coming."*

## Appendix: Proposed Hero Section Rewrite (Final Version)

**Current:**
> "Explore 150 years of macroeconomic history across 18 countries. Scroll through interactive storylines to discover the patterns that shape our economies."

**Proposed:**
> **Every financial crisis in history was preceded by the same warning signs.**
>
> Rapid lending. Rising asset prices. Widespread belief that things were different this time.
>
> They were never different. This is the story of why — told through 150 years of data, across 18 countries, from 1870 to the present.

*(Subheading:)*
> Four chapters. Four forces that have shaped — and repeatedly shaken — the modern economy.

This version creates urgency, establishes a thesis, and promises both narrative and evidence. It respects the reader's intelligence while making the stakes clear.
